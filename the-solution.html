How to Stop
Overpaying
for TV

You don't have to cancel everything. You just need a strategy. Here's a practical, research-backed guide to getting the most TV for the least money.

Your Action Plan

5 Steps to a Smarter Streaming Setup

01
Audit First

Know What You're Actually Paying

Before you cancel anything, list every single subscription. Check your bank statements — most people are paying for 1–3 services they forgot about or rarely use. The average American household pays for 4–5 streaming services. At $15/month each, that's $60–$75/month before cable, internet, or live TV services.

Write it down: name of service, monthly cost, when you last watched it. If you haven't used it in 30 days, it's a candidate for cancellation.

02
Check Overlap

Are You Paying for the Same Content Twice?

Research by Colin Dixon (NscreenMedia, 2021) found that Hulu users can access over 21% of Peacock's TV catalog — meaning if you have Hulu, there's a 21% chance that a show you want on Peacock is already available to you for free. Disney+ and Hulu have almost zero overlap, so those might both be worth keeping.

The rule: if two services share a large chunk of content, keep the one with the bigger library. The smaller-catalog service is likely redundant.

03
Go Free or Ad-Supported

Use Free Tiers Before Paying for Anything

Services like Tubi, Pluto TV, and The Roku Channel offer thousands of movies and TV shows completely free, supported by ads (just like regular cable). For casual viewing, these platforms are genuinely solid — especially for older content, classic TV, and background watching.

The Boardroom analysis notes: "For budget-conscious viewers, these platforms serve as a reminder that not every screen in the house requires a $15 subscription." Try free first. Only upgrade if you're actively missing content you care about.

04
Bundle Smarter

Buy Bundles, Not Individual Services

Some of the best values in streaming come from bundles. The Disney Bundle (Disney+, Hulu, ESPN+) costs significantly less than subscribing to each individually. As CNET's 2026 streaming review notes, "One of the best offerings available right now is the HBO Max-Disney package, but new bundles are constantly being introduced."

Always check if a bundle includes the services you already pay for separately — you could be leaving monthly savings on the table.

05
Rotate, Don't Subscribe

Watch, Then Cancel. Repeat.

Most streaming services have no contract — you can cancel anytime. A smart strategy is "binge and bounce": subscribe for one month to watch a specific season or series, then cancel until the next season drops. Services count on your passive subscription income. Don't give it to them.

You can also time free trials around major releases. Many services still offer 7–30 day free trials, especially for new subscribers.

What matters most to you when watching TV?

Your Pick: YouTube TV or Hulu + Live TV

For live sports and news, a live TV streaming service is your best bet. YouTube TV (~$73/mo) or Hulu + Live TV (~$83/mo) give you the cable experience — including local channels, ESPN, and sports networks — without the cable contract. Yes, it's expensive, but it's comparable to a real cable package and you can cancel anytime.

Your Pick: Netflix + One More

Netflix remains the leader for original content and has the widest overall library. Pair it with Max (for HBO quality shows) and you'll cover the vast majority of acclaimed TV and film. Total cost: around $32–$36/month. Skip the rest until something specific catches your eye, then subscribe and cancel after you watch it.

Your Pick: Tubi + Pluto TV (Free)

Start with Tubi and Pluto TV — both completely free with ads. Together they provide tens of thousands of titles and live TV channels. If you find yourself wanting more after a month, consider adding just one paid service (Netflix or Disney+ at the cheapest ad-supported tier). You can watch a lot of great content without spending a dollar.

Your Pick: Disney Bundle

The Disney Bundle (Disney+, Hulu, ESPN+) is the single best value for families. Disney+ alone has Pixar, Marvel, Star Wars, and National Geographic for kids of all ages. Hulu adds general entertainment and Hulu Originals. ESPN+ satisfies adult sports fans. The bundle costs $14–$25/month depending on ad tier — far less than buying each separately.
