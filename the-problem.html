How Did
TV Get So

From a revolutionary alternative to cable to a fragmented, monopoly-driven market, the story of how streaming lost its promise, and what it costs you today.

A Brief History

The Rise (and struggle) of Streaming

Streaming didn't start out expensive. Understanding how it got here helps us make smarter choices.

Early 2000s

YouTube Changes Everything

Before streaming TV existed, YouTube pioneered the idea that the internet could deliver video at scale. Its success pushed Netflix to transition from a DVD-by-mail service into the streaming giant we know today. The streaming era had begun.

2010s

The Golden Age of Streaming

Netflix, Hulu, and Amazon Prime offered affordable access to massive libraries of content. The "cut the cord" movement took off, and people were paying $8–$10/month instead of $100+ for cable. It felt like a permanent victory for consumers.

2019–2021

The Streaming Wars Begin

Disney+, HBO Max, Peacock, Paramount+, and Apple TV+ all launched within a short window. Suddenly, every major studio had its own service, pulling its content away from Netflix. The market became fragmented; if you wanted everything, you had to pay for everything.

2022–2023

Password Crackdowns & Price Hikes

As subscriber growth plateaued, Netflix cracked down on password sharing, frustrating millions of users. Services across the board raised prices and introduced cheaper, ad-supported tiers. The affordable era was officially over.

2025–2026

Consolidation & $100 Billion in Spending

Streaming service spending hit $100 billion in 2026, and those costs flow directly to you, the subscriber. Netflix acquired sports rights, Disney absorbed Hulu, and Netflix struck a deal with Warner Bros. The few companies left are getting bigger and more expensive.

Corporate Consolidation

Who Actually Controls What You Watch

A small number of massive corporations now own nearly every major streaming service, studio, and content library. This eliminates competition, which means no pressure to keep prices fair.

"By Q2 of 2023, Amazon held 21% of the U.S. streaming market and Netflix held 20%. Disney has 13% and Hulu 11%. That's over 65% of the entire market controlled by just three parent companies."
— ProMarket, "How Monopolies are Making TV Worse" (Nov. 2023)

The Sports Problem

Sports: The Most Expensive Content War

Live sports were cable's last stronghold — the one thing streaming couldn't replicate. That changed fast. Now, to watch every major sport live, you need multiple separate subscriptions. Here's roughly what full sports coverage costs in 2025–2026:

Netflix
$17.99/mo — NFL, WWE RAW
ESPN+
$11.99/mo — College, MMA, some MLB
Peacock
$7.99/mo — NFL, Premier League, Olympics
Paramount+
$5.99/mo — NFL, UEFA Champions League
Amazon Prime
$8.99/mo — NFL Thursday Night
YouTube TV
$72.99/mo — Most live sports & local channels

The hard truth: If you want full live sports coverage across all major leagues, you're likely spending $50–$100+/month just on sports-capable services. This is the same price as cable — but you're managing 5 separate apps and 5 separate bills. As Nick Bythrow reports in ScreenRant, sports acquisition is the #1 driver of streaming cost increases in 2026.
